A key technique — before we start

Read this carefully. It will help you with the last question.

Sometimes we need to rewrite a number in scientific notation — changing the coefficient and the exponent — without changing the actual value. Here is the rule:

The Rescaling Rule:
If you move the decimal in the coefficient one place to the right (which multiplies it by 10), you must decrease the exponent by 1 to keep the number exactly the same.

Example 1  — Positive exponent

Start 4.2 × 10⁶
→ Move decimal right: coefficient becomes 42
→ Exponent drops by 1: 6 − 1 = 5, so we get 10⁵
✓ 4.2 × 10⁶ = 42 × 10⁵

Example 2  — Negative exponent

Start 4.2 × 10⁻⁶
→ Move decimal right: coefficient becomes 42
→ Exponent drops by 1: −6 − 1 = −7, so we get 10⁻⁷
✓ 4.2 × 10⁻⁶ = 42 × 10⁻⁷

Why does this matter?  — Division of scientific notation

Rescaling helps when dividing gives a messy coefficient. Instead of getting a decimal you then have to normalise, you can rescale first so the division comes out clean.

Problem (4.2 × 10⁷) ÷ (6 × 10³)
→ 4.2 ÷ 6 = 0.7 — messy! Rescale the top number instead:
→ 4.2 × 10⁷ = 42 × 10⁶  (decimal right, exponent drops by 1)
→ Now divide: 42 ÷ 6 = 7  (clean!),  10⁶ ÷ 10³ = 10³
✓ Answer: 7 × 10³
